The NFL Draft: calculated maneuvers or blind faith?

Once a year, NFL teams have the opportunity to acquire young, talented players for a reasonable price tag. General manangers have a laundry list of responsibilies, but the annual infusion of youth can be used as a measuring stick for their success.

Project by Braden Reed

Every April, 259 NFL hopefuls hear their name called on draft day.

An Overview

With the 1st overall pick in the 2021 NFL Draft, the Jacksonville Jaguars select Trevor Lawrence, quarterback, Clemson.

And so began three days of madness in Cleveland, Ohio, where 32 NFL teams took their pick of incoming players to add to their teams. The NFL Draft has become a public spectacle in recent years, the completely virtual 2020 draft set the record for TV viewership.

Back at team offices, executives do their best to choose who they project to be the future of the league. Some teams have more success doing so than others, which tends to lead to more success for those franchises.

Succeeding in the draft makes life a lot easier for NFL front offices. Since the players drafted are on more affordable contracts, the money saved can be spent on retaining the best players on the team or going out and signing talented free agents on the open market.

A perfect example of this is Seattle Seahawks quarterback Russell Wilson. Wilson was drafted in the 3rd round in 2012, and was being paid less than one million dollars when he led the Seahawks to Super Bowl appearances in both 2012 and 2013. Instead of having to spend north of $30 million for a top quarterback in the league, Seattle was able to spend more money on important positions like a Pro Bowl secondary of Richard Sherman, Kam Chancellor, and Earl Thomas. Because of this, the ‘Legion of Boom’ Seattle defense was the best unit in the NFL for four straight seasons.

On the other side of the coin, the Philadelphia Eagles have struggled to identify talented college players in recent years. With a glaring need at the receiver position, the Eagles spent a 2nd round pick on JJ Arcega-Whiteside. Seven picks later, the aforementioned Seahawks selected DK Metcalf, who was named to a Pro Bowl in his second season. Arcega-Whiteside has 16 career catches.

A year later, the Eagles still had a hole. In the first round this time, Philly selected Jalen Reagor. With the very next pick, the Minnesota Vikings selected Justin Jefferson. Jefferson was named to the NFL All-Pro team and broke the rookie receiving yards record. Reagor dropped two game-winning touchdowns in Week 12 this season.

It took until 2021 for the Eagles to finally get it right. They selected Heisman trophy-winner DeVonta Smith in the first round, and he looks to be the number one receiver that they so desperately needed.

Philly may have finally gotten their guy, but it took them two first round picks and a second round pick to do so. That's a lot of capital. That's why the draft is so important to team success. If the Eagles got it right the first time, those two first round picks could've been spent filling out other holes on the roster.

The NFL Draft order is decided by reverse order of the previous season's standings. Whichever team finishes with the worst record in the league is granted the #1 pick, and whichever team wins the Super Bowl picks #32. This order applies to rounds 1-7. Teams are also granted what are called compensatory picks based on players that left to sign with a different team. Those picks fall between rounds 3-7. All of the picks allocated for each draft add up to 259 total selections.
